Optimal Hedging with Advanced Delta Modelling

CQF Final Project ‐ June 2023 cohort

Volatility arbitrage

When the actual volatility exceeds the implied volatility, implementing delta hedging on a long option position can guarantee a profit. The expected value of this profit is dependent on the hedging approach employed. It is demonstrated that hedging with the delta derived from actual volatility results in a deterministic profit, whereas the profit obtained by hedging with implied volatility is uncertain and path-dependent. These findings have been the subject of numerous studies in the literature, including Ahmad and Wilmott [1]. The results are proven both analytically and numerically, utilizing Monte Carlo simulations to model the asset price evolution. Additionally, the path dependency of the profit when hedging with implied volatility is broken down in terms of the Greeks.

Minimum Variance Delta

Option chains for the S&P500 index are analyzed to develop a mathematical model for determining the Minimum Variance Delta that improves hedging performance by minimizing the variance of hedging errors. Historical data are employed to estimate the parameters involved in the mathematical model, following the approach demonstrated by Hull and White[2].

References

  1. Riaz Ahmad and Paul Wilmott. Which Free Lunch Would You Like Today, Sir? Delta Hedging, Volatility Arbitrage and Optimal Portfolios. Wilmott Magazine, pages 64–79, 01 2005.
  2. John Hull and Alan White. Optimal delta hedging for options. Journal of Banking & Finance, 82:180–190, 2017.
